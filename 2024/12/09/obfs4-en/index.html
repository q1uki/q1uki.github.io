【Paper Reading】"Detecting probe resistant proxy": Experimental Verification and Analysis

Introduction

When I first encountered this paper on proxy detection, I noticed a lack of Chinese discussion on it. I have compiled my notes after reading and experimenting (focusing on Tor’s obfs4), to facilitate learning and discussion for those who might need it.
(Now obfs4 is becoming obsolete, with new WebTunnels being introduced, but upgrades and iterations still take time.)

Paper Reading

Probe Selection

Our attack focuses on the observation that not responding with data is an anomaly on the internet. By sending probes consisting of popular protocols and random data, we can elicit responses from almost all endpoints (94%) in the Tap dataset of over 400k IP/port pairs.
For endpoints that do not respond, proxy-specific TCP behaviors (such as timeouts and data thresholds) differ from other servers.

Starting with 6 basic probes: HTTP, TLS, MODBUS, S7, random bytes, and an empty probe that sends no data after connection. For each probe, we record how the server responds with data (if any), when it closes the connection (if any), and how it closes the connection (TCP FIN or RST).

  • HTTP: For HTTP, we send a simple HTTP/1.1 GET request with a host header of example.com. Most servers will respond with a redirect or error page. Even non-HTTP servers may respond to this probe with error messages inherent to their protocol.
  • TLS: We send a TLS Client Hello message, typically generated by a Chromium version 71 browser. This includes popular cipher suites and extensions, and even if the server does not support TLS, it should respond with a TLS alert message.
  • Modbus: Modbus is commonly used in Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) environments for Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs) and other embedded devices. We use the probe defined in ZGrab, which sends a 3-byte command to request device information descriptors from the remote host.
  • S7: S7 is a proprietary protocol used by Siemens PLC devices. Again, we use the probe defined in ZGrab, which sends a device identifier request via the COTP/S7 protocol.
  • Random Bytes: Probes with varying amounts of random bytes. Servers will fail to parse them and respond with an error message, or close the connection in a manner distinct from proxy servers.
  • Empty Probe: Does not send data after connection. Some protocols (like SSH) have the server communicate first (or simultaneously) with the client. For other protocols, implementations might have different timeouts when the client sends some initial data compared to when it sends no data.
  • DNS AXFR: Constructed a DNS AXFR (Zone Transfer) query probe based on the DNS specification.
  • STUN: Implemented a STUN probe based on the ZGrab-Golang library to probe Cisco WebEx devices.

Experimental Data

  • Proxy Endpoints: Collected over 20 obfs4 proxy endpoints from Tor’s BridgeDB.
  • Non-Proxy Endpoints: Used active network scans from ZMap and passive collection of network flow data. We used ZMap to send a SYN packet to 20,000 hosts on every TCP port (0-65535), totaling 1.3 billion probes. We found 1.5 million endpoints that responded with SYN ACK, which we labeled as the ZMap dataset. Sampled netflow data from the University of Colorado’s 10 Gbps gateway to collect endpoints. In countries without network censorship, the vast majority of traffic does not involve proxies, so the collected endpoints are primarily other services. Over a 3-day span, we collected over 550,000 server IP/port nodes from the ISP. Of these, 433,286 (79%) hosts accepted connections (responding with a TCP SYN-ACK), while most of the remainder simply timed out when connection was attempted. Subsequent scans were conducted within days of observing connections, and some servers may have moved IPs or gone offline during this time. Secondly, servers may have firewalls configured to only allow access from certain IPs, potentially blocking ZMap scans. Ultimately, over 400,000 data-sending servers were captured in this dataset.

Experimental Results

We sent 13 probes (7 from Section III-A and 6 with random data ranging from 4KB-40KB) to 1.9 million nodes and recorded whether and when the server responded with data or closed the connection. If the server closed, we recorded whether it used FIN or RST. If the server did not respond or close the connection, we timed out after 300 seconds and labeled the result as a timeout. We recorded their closing thresholds.

Since anti-probe proxies never respond with data, responsive endpoints can be discarded. In the passive Tap dataset, this eliminated 94% of hosts, leaving only 26,021 potential proxies. In the ZMap dataset, the vast majority of hosts had no data response to any probe, discarding only 1.16% of endpoints.

The reason is that among the nodes identified by ZMap, firewalls used a chaff strategy, only responding to SYN on specific subnets. 42% of nodes behaved identically, neither sending data nor closing connections to the probe. Clustering response data (response type, number of response bytes, connection close time, or timeout time). In the Tap dataset, the most popular response group contained 3% of endpoints, appearing to be TLS servers within the Cloudflare network (99.9% on endpoints with port 443). Figure 2 shows the CDFs of different response sets (ordered by popularity) for the ZMap and Tap datasets, illustrating greater diversity in the Tap dataset. The top 10 response sets accounted for over 80% of endpoints in the ZMap dataset, but only 13% of endpoints collected in Tap.

Detection Methods

  1. Buffering Thresholds: Servers will close with FIN after receiving a certain number of bytes, and may close with RST after receiving a higher number of bytes. The closing thresholds of proxy services are mostly unique. Consider a server that reads N bytes from a client and attempts to parse them as a protocol header. If parsing fails (e.g., invalid field, checksum, or MAC), the server can simply close the connection. If the client sends only N-1 bytes, the server might keep the connection open and wait for additional data before attempting to parse.

The obfs4 handshake can be up to 8192 bytes long, so the server reads this many bytes before determining the client is invalid and entering the closeAfterDelay function. This function closes the connection after a random delay (30-90 seconds), or after the server reads an additional N bytes (N randomly chosen between 0 and 8192) at server startup. However, these reads are all done using a 1448-byte buffer. This means if 8192+N bytes are sent, the obfs4 server will send a FIN, and if 8192 + N - ((8192 + N) mod 1448) + 1448 bytes are sent, it will return an RST. This is obfs4’s unique behavior.

After the paper authors communicated their research findings with torproject, obfs4proxy has fixed the closing threshold behavior in version 0.0.11.
2. Timeout Durations: Many endpoints have different timeouts based on the amount of data they receive. Hosts that do not acknowledge data are not standard TCP behavior; we retransmit, and proxies do not exhibit this behavior, so it does not affect the results.

The closing threshold was fixed, but timeout behavior can still be observed.
3. Rare Responses: Not responding to any probe is rare.

Data Content

Involves algorithm: curve25519

Both client and server must have their own Keypair instances. As defined in the curve25519 package, PrivateKey is randomly generated within a certain range. According to the ECC algorithm, PublicKey can be generated from the private key by calling curve25519.ScalarBaseMult().

The Representative key is generated from the public key and can be converted back to the public key by calling extra25519.RepresentativeToPublicKey() when needed. The definition and initialization code for Keypair are located in the NewKeypair() function, defined in the ntor.go file.

Using ScalarBaseMult() from extra25519.go, generate public and representative keys from the privatekey. The representative key is obscured using Elligator 2 mapping to prevent inference of the encryption algorithm after collecting a significant number of public keys.
Algorithm detailed description

Both the client and server should keep their private keys secure and send their public keys to each other in the form of Representative keys. The obfs4 client’s connection process begins with the handshake message, so let’s examine the client’s handshake message. The overall structure of this message is as follows:

  1. Keypair.representative, with a length of 20h bytes. On the server side, it can be used to restore the client’s public key. (obfs4 bridge configuration line)
  2. Padding data using random bytes, with a size ranging from 4Dh to 1FC0h. This padding data obfuscates the size of the handshake packet, making it harder to identify.
  3. The HMAC value of the first part, Keypair.representative. Obfs4 uses SHA-256 to generate the HMAC, with a length of 20h bytes. Obfs4 only keeps the first 10h bytes as HMAC, and the remaining 10h bytes are discarded.
  4. Obfs4 uses the current system time to calculate the hour value of the UNIX Epoch time (starting from UTC 1970-01-01 Thursday 00:00:00). It calculates the HMAC value of the first three parts of the packet, plus (meaning concatenated with) the hour value in the string. Similarly, the result has a length of 20h bytes, with the first 10h bytes serving as the fourth part of the packet. (Replay protection)

obfs4proxy Source Code Functions

obfs4.go

  • WrapConn(): Calls serverHandshake(). Regardless of the error returned, it calls closeAfterDelay().
  • serverHandshake(): Creates a new server handshake, setting a baseline timeout. Then, it sets a buffer for the maximum readable length (8192), reads the incoming packet, saves its content, and then parseClientHandshake() parses the content. If the parsing error is ErrMarkNotFoundYet, it continues to read packets; otherwise, the function returns an error. If parsing is successful, the buffer is cleared.

handshake_ntor.go

  • parseClientHandshake(): For too little received content / received content length not reaching the upper limit, or if MarkMAC is not found, an ErrMarkNotFoundYet error handling is prepared, allowing it to return and continue reading. All other errors close the connection. If markMAC/HMAC is not found, or packet length is non-compliant (contains data not needed for handshake), ErrInvalidHandshake is returned. For replay, ErrReplayedHandshake is returned. If calculation verification fails, ErrNtorFailed is returned.
  • closeAfterDelay(): closeAfterDelay() has been modified, fixing the vulnerability of generating FIN and RST due to thresholds. Older versions would terminate the connection after a 30s delay / receiving a certain length of data, while new versions always terminate after a 30s delay.
  • SetReadDeadline(): 30s after starting to read data, any subsequent incoming data will also return a timeout error and terminate the connection. (The paper mentions a random delay, which I couldn’t find).

obfs4proxy.go

  • serverHandler():
    Calls WrapConn(). If an error is returned, logs the handshake failure.
  • serverAcceptLoop():
    Makes the server loop to initiate handshakes with clients, calling serverHandler().

Analysis and Experiment

Paper Analysis

In the paper’s manually constructed obfs4 decision tree, it first sends 17k bytes of random data. If an RST is returned within 2s, it proceeds to the next step, sending other probes. If a FIN is returned within 30s–90s, it is identified as obfs4. The 17k data was meant to trigger the threshold issue, which has been fixed, but the delayed FIN situation still exists.

The experimental data for obfs4 bridges in the paper was too scarce, so I decided to conduct my own experimental verification. After testing several known obfs4 bridges, I coincidentally found that each bridge had a fixed timeout duration.

Conjecture: Each bridge has a fixed timeout duration. (Could the bridge change after service restart? Is the random delay time generated only once?)
I tested 6 known obfs4 bridges, sending the same data (replaying data from a successful handshake initiated by the Tor browser). This hit the “verification failed” error, leading to closeAfterDelay().
I initiated two handshakes for each bridge. For the same bridge, from two different IP clients, both handshakes returned FIN after the same duration, both falling within [30, 90]s.

Experimental Verification

  1. Test Set: After filtering the test set, I obtained over 400 active services.
  2. Judgment Criteria: TLS and HTTP probes receive no response, and 17k bytes return RST or timeout duration (returning FIN) is within [30, 90]s.
  3. Experimental Results:
    ① 80% of services were identified as obfs4 bridges. Among these, 50% returned RST, and 50% had timeout durations within the specified range.
    ② 6% of services were identified as not obfs4 bridges.
    ③ 10% of services could not be determined; sending 17k bytes immediately received a FIN, and both TLS and HTTP timed out.

Experimental Analysis

  1. 80% of bridge services met the judgment criteria, verifying the feasibility of the criteria.
  2. Among the 6% of services, sending TLS and HTTP both received responses, indicating other services could be detected. This is consistent with expectations that the provided service changed.
  3. The remaining 10% of services did not meet the judgment criteria. However, after picking a few and configuring them in the Tor browser, they could be used as bridges. After sending 20k bytes, 50% returned RST. After replaying the obfs4 handshake data, all returned FIN within [30, 90]s. It is speculated that these are bridge services that have already upgraded to newer versions. After replaying handshake data, verification fails, and they enter the delayed FIN function. However, 20k already exceeds the RST threshold observed in the paper, and later sending 30k only resulted in a few responses with RST.

Experimental Conclusions

  1. Sending sufficiently large random data immediately returns RST (fixed in obfs4proxy 0.0.11).
  2. Sending data matching the handshake length returns FIN within [30, 90]s.
